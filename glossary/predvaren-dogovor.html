Предварителен Договор (Preliminary Contract)

Bulgaria's preliminary property agreement — establishes terms before notarization and final registration.

Overview

A Предварителен Договор (Preliminary Agreement) is the initial contract between a property buyer and seller establishing essential transaction terms. It outlines property identification, price, payment schedule, delivery date, contingencies (financing, inspection), and timeline for notarization and registration. The preliminary contract creates binding obligations between parties but does not transfer legal ownership, which occurs only through notarization and registration in the Gruntovnica.

The preliminary contract should clearly identify the property using cadastral data, specify the purchase price and payment terms (including any deposits), establish timelines for mortgage approval and property inspection, describe the property's condition, and identify contingencies allowing party withdrawal under specific circumstances. Both parties should have the agreement reviewed by a lawyer to ensure proper protection.

Typical timelines in preliminary agreements provide 30-60 days for mortgage financing approval, property inspection, and legal review, with final notarization and registration scheduled thereafter. Early termination clauses should specify remedies if either party breaches (forfeiture of deposits, damages claims). Clear documentation of deadlines prevents disputes and protects both parties.

The preliminary contract is important evidence of the parties' original intent and may be referenced if disputes arise about transaction circumstances or unperformed obligations. While the preliminary contract creates obligations, actual ownership transfer requires the notarized Property Sale Contract (Договор за Продажба) and registration in the Property Registry.

Bulgarian law permits enforcement of preliminary contract terms through courts. If the seller refuses to proceed after receipt of the buyer's deposit, the buyer may sue for specific performance or damages. Conversely, if the buyer breaches without legal cause, the seller may retain the deposit and sue for additional damages.

Key Facts

  • Initial agreement establishing transaction terms before notarization
  • Should include property identification, price, payment terms, and timeline
  • Includes contingencies for financing, inspection, and legal review
  • Creates contractual obligations but does not transfer ownership
  • Ownership transfers only upon notarization and Gruntovnica registration

Common Mistake

Treating the preliminary contract as final — legal transfer occurs only through the notarized final contract and registration.

Pro Tip

Include specific dates and contingency conditions in the preliminary agreement to avoid ambiguity and provide clear enforcement mechanisms.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a written preliminary contract necessary in Bulgaria?

While not always legally required, a written preliminary contract is essential. It documents party intentions, protects both parties, and serves as evidence if disputes arise. Most mortgage lenders require one.

What happens if the buyer cannot obtain mortgage financing?

If the preliminary contract includes a financing contingency, the buyer can withdraw without penalty if mortgage approval is denied. Without this clause, the buyer may breach the contract and be liable for damages or forfeiture of deposits.

Can either party cancel a preliminary contract?

Cancellation is possible only with both parties' agreement or if specific contract conditions (contingencies) are not met. Unilateral cancellation without legal cause constitutes breach of contract.
