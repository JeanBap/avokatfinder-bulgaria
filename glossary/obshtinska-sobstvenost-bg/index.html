Obshtinska Sobstvenost (Municipal Property)

Municipal property (obshtinska sobstvenost) in Bulgaria is real estate owned by a municipality. Some can be purchased by private buyers via tender, but restrictions apply to certain categories, and agricultural land is subject to additional rules.

Bulgarian municipalities own significant stocks of real estate — land, buildings, and apartments — classified as obshtinska sobstvenost (municipal property). This is distinct from darzhavna sobstvenost (state property, owned by the central government). Both categories can in some circumstances be sold to private buyers, but the process is regulated and subject to restrictions.

Municipal property can be sold through public tender (publichno targovaane), auction (targ), or in some cases direct negotiation where legally permitted. The selling municipality must adopt a formal decision (reshenie) of its council, and the transaction must be at not less than the officially assessed value. All such sales are published in local official gazettes and on the municipality's website.

Buyers of former municipal flats (sold to their occupants in the 1990s under the privatisation laws) should be aware that some such properties have restitution claims against them — the original pre-communist owner or their heirs may have submitted a claim for return of the property. Restitution claims are a significant Bulgarian property risk that requires specific due diligence.

Agricultural land in Bulgaria has additional ownership restrictions: non-EU nationals and companies with majority non-EU ownership cannot purchase agricultural land. EU nationals have had the right to purchase Bulgarian agricultural land without restriction since 2014 (the end of the EU accession transition period).

The right to build on municipal land (pravo na stroezh — right of construction/superficies) is sometimes granted to private persons rather than ownership of the land itself. Buyers of buildings on municipal land should carefully check whether they own the land or hold a pravo na stroezh, as the latter is limited in duration and may affect resale and mortgage options.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a flat that was previously municipal property without checking for active restitution claims (iskove za restitutsia) is a significant risk in Bulgaria. Restitution court proceedings can be lengthy and can result in the original owner recovering the property.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Before buying any property with a history of state or municipal ownership, instruct your lawyer to search the restitution register and any pending court claims attached to the property in the Imotni Registar.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a restitution claim?

A claim by former property owners (or their heirs) whose property was nationalised under communism to have it returned under post-1989 restitution laws. Active claims are often annotated in the Imotni Registar.

What is pravo na stroezh?

The right to construct and own a building on land owned by another party (municipality or state). The right is time-limited — typically 99 years from grant. When it expires, the building reverts to the land owner unless renewed.

Can an EU citizen buy agricultural land in Bulgaria?

Yes, since Bulgaria fully opened its agricultural land market to EU nationals in 2014. Non-EU nationals still face restrictions and typically must use a Bulgarian-registered company to hold agricultural land.
