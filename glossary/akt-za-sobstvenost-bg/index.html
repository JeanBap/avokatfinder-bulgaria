Akt za Sobstvenost (Title Certificate / Act of Ownership)

The akt za sobstvenost is a Bulgarian document certifying property ownership — most commonly the original notarial deed (notarialen akt), but also including court-issued acts and administrative title certificates for state-allocated property.

In Bulgarian property law, the akt za sobstvenost (act of ownership or title document) is the generic term for any document evidencing the owner's right to a property. The most common form is the notarialen akt (notarial deed of purchase), but the term also covers: deeds of inheritance (notarialen akt za nasledstvo), gifts (darestvena), court settlement acts (sadebn akt), and administrative allocation acts for state or municipal property distributed in the early post-communist period.

Administrative allocation acts from the 1990s — when state flats were sold to their occupants at low prices — are still in circulation as title documents for many Bulgarian apartments, particularly in the former socialist housing blocks (panelki). These acts have full legal validity but were often not cross-referenced with accurate cadastral data, creating boundary and area discrepancies that persist today.

When purchasing, always verify that the akt za sobstvenost the seller holds matches the current Imotni Registar entry and the AGKK cadastral record. Discrepancies in area, owner identity, or property description must be resolved before exchange — they cannot be assumed away.

Lost title documents can be recovered by obtaining a certified copy of the original notarial deed from the notary's archives (notarni arhivi) — notarial records are maintained indefinitely. The Registration Agency also holds copies of registered deeds.

Bulgaria does not have a Torrens-style system where registration alone guarantees title. Ownership disputes resolved by court can override the Imotni Registar entry — making full due diligence on the ownership chain essential.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Accepting a 1990s administrative allocation act as the only title document without verifying it against current cadastral records is risky. Floor area stated in such acts is frequently incorrect — affecting mortgage valuations and future sale value.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Cross-check the area stated in the akt za sobstvenost against the AGKK cadastral record. If they differ, establish which is correct before exchange — the cadastral area prevails for most practical purposes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a notarialen akt za nasledstvo?

A notarial deed of inheritance issued when an heir formally accepts and registers their inherited property. It is the Bulgarian equivalent of a grant of probate for real estate and must be registered in the Imotni Registar.

Can a Bulgarian property have multiple akts za sobstvenost?

It can have multiple historical title documents, but at any time only one registered entry in the Imotni Registar records the current owner. Earlier acts are superseded by later registrations but should still be reviewed to trace the chain of title.

Is the notarial deed sufficient for mortgage purposes?

Yes. Banks accept the notarialen akt as the primary title evidence. They also require the Imotni Registar extract and AGKK cadastral certificate.
